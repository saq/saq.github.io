Tropical Field Ecology (Field trip Course)

F&ES 717b, E&EB 617

Credits: 3 | Spring 2016 | Tu, 3:30-4:50, 201 OML

This course is intended to give students first-hand knowledge of tropical biology and the issues surrounding conservation of biodiversity in a developing nation. It does so in the context of an intensive foreign study tour in Ecuador. The course includes travel to different tropical habitats, independent field projects, written and oral presentation of results, guided natural history exploration, and interaction with local and international scientists.

Personnel

Instructors: Dr. Simon Queenborough (F&ES) and Dr. Walter Jetz (EEB)

Teaching Fellow: Andrew Muehleisen

Overview

The course meets in Spring 2017, Tuesdays 3:30 -4:50pm, with a mandatory field trip to Ecuador over Spring Break (tentative field trip dates: March 12-23).

This course provides a broad overview of the ecology, conservation and management of tropical habitats, with an emphasis on active learning and developing independent research projects carried out during the field trip. Using a case study approach, topics covered will include patterns of biodiversity, tropical forest structure and function, reforestation, species interactions and coevolution, climate change, ecosystem services and human land use. We will visit a variety of tropical ecosystems and hear first-hand from scientists about current research in the field.

Students will undertake two short-term research projects, and also learn basic identification and natural history of tropical plant, bird, mammal and insect species. The course will include weekly seminars on-campus and a mandatory field trip during Spring Break in Ecuador, a country famous for its high biological, cultural and economic diversity.

Important Notice

This is a course in field ecology in a foreign country. Students should expect to spend most of each day outside in the natural tropical environment under adverse conditions. A good degree of base physical fitness is expected and all typical health preparations for visit to a tropical country are required. It will be hot and humid. There will be biting insects. There is a (small) risk of serious illness. Ecuadorian culture is very different from the US.

Learning Goals & Outcomes

Successful students will be able to:

  1. foundational knowledge (information and ideas)
  • Describe key features of the common families of tropical plants,
  • Describe key features of the common families of tropical birds and other terrestrial vertebrates and of major insects groups
  • Explain how the environment, historical biogeography, and species interactions influence species distribution and abundances
  • Describe the variety of biotic interactions in tropical forests, and how they influence diversity,
  • Explain how these varied determinants of species diversity in turn affect the management of biodiversity in changing landscapes.
  • Explain how human agriculture and other activities have affected natural ecosystems in Ecuador,
  • Describe the development and effect of the petroleum exploitation Ecuadorian society and ecosystems.
  1. application (skills, thinking and project management)
  • Critically evaluate the scientific literature and applied conservation reports
  • Develop hypotheses
  • Design high quality experiments and analyse the data
  1. integration (connecting ideas)
  • Connect evolutionary underpinnings of biology and ecology
  • Understand the interactions between humans and nature in tropical ecosystems.
  1. human dimension (learning about oneself and others)
  • Understand the effect of humans on evolution and ecology,
  • Educate and inform others about ecology and conservation,
  • Appreciate different viewpoints on conservation and development.
  1. values (developing new feelings, interests and values)
  • Value evidence and the scientific method,
  • Develop and retain a sense of wonder of and value for the natural world.
  1. learning how to learn (becoming a better student and self-directed learner)
  • Know how to read and present material clearly and concisely,
  • Plan how to best answer hypotheses and prepare papers and presentation,
  • Strengthen areas of weakness in your learning abilities.

Assessment

Students will be assessed through the following:

  1. Collection or photography and identification animals and plants. (10%)

  2. Development and execution of part of a class project comparing different forest ecosystems in Ecuador. (15% oral presentation, 15% written paper)
  • Students will work in small groups to compare the diversity and distribution of taxa across the elevational range and climate/ecosystem gradient (birds, insects, or plants, half a day at each site).
  1. An independent research project in Ecuador. (15% oral presentation, 25% written paper)
  • Students can work alone or in small groups on a project of their choosing at Yasuni. Group projects must be written up individually.

  1. Peer-review of the papers. (10%)

  2. General conduct/participation in Ecuador. (10%)
